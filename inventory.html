Introduction

"Mathematics is the language in which God has written the universe." This quote basically means that there is some sort of math involved in literally everything you see around you. On this website, I’m diving into some in-depth research to show you how folding a simple square of paper involves far more math than you might expect.

Origami has been a hobby of mine since childhood. I’ve always loved the process of crafting tangible things: frogs, fish, cranes, strawberries, etc, out of nothing but paper. Having an artistic hobby helps me stay grounded and balances out the stress of my academic life. As a prospective double major in Computer Science and Statistics, I have a natural affinity for math and computational problem-solving. This project is my way of bringing those two worlds together: I'm essentially merging my passion for mathematics with the hands-on art of paper folding.

This inventory serves as a roadmap for the project, detailing how I will categorize familiar models: like the jumping frog and the classic crane, into three approachable mathematical domains: Symmetry and Proportionality, Point-and-Line Geometry, and 3D Space Logic. For the data section, I’ll be using a "Crease Counter" visualization to track the complexity of each model. Instead of dense tables, the site will feature "crease counts" and simplified diagrams that map out the total number of folds required to reach each finished shape. By using straightforward digital tools to calculate these intersections and physical paper to verify the results, I plan to show that even a simple childhood hobby is actually a clever system of physical math in action.

Images

The images here are all my own origami creations, divided into the following categories:

1. Symmetry & Proportionality

This category focuses on the balance of the paper. These models usually involve folding the square into equal halves or quarters, relying on reflection where whatever you do to the left side, you must do to the right.

  • The Math: This is basically Euclidean Geometry. It is about congruence (making two shapes identical) and ratios.
  • The Models: The Samurai Hat, the Cat, the Origami Butterfly, and the Heart. They are perfect examples because they look exactly the same on both sides and use simple angles.

2. Point-and-Line Geometry

This is where we look at the Crease Pattern as a roadmap. Instead of just folding in half, we are creating specific intersection points where multiple lines meet. This is the Computer Science heart of origami, as it deals with the rules of what can actually be folded flat.

  • The Math: This involves Graph Theory and Kawasaki’s Theorem. We use the "Crease Counter" here to show how many vertices (points) and edges (lines) it takes to build a base.
  • The Models: The Jumping Frog, the Fortune Teller, the Origami star, and the kite fold. These rely on a central base where all the action happens at a few specific points in the middle of the paper.

3. Topological Transformations

This is the most advanced category. It is about how a flat, 2D sheet of paper is transformed into a 3D object with depth and layers. It deals with how paper can be tucked, reversed, or squashed without ever being cut or glued.

  • The Math: This is Topology and 3D Kinematics. It studies how the surface area is redistributed; for example, how a large square becomes a thin, narrow neck for a bird.
  • The Models: The Crane, the fish, the paper plane, and the Paper Boat. These models involve petal folds or inside-reverse folds that change the dimensionality of the paper, creating layers that stack on top of each other.

Data

To quantify the complexity of each model, I developed a data collection method called the Crease Counter. This process involves unfolding each completed physical model and mapping the resulting creases onto a digital grid. I then categorize and count every individual fold line, distinguishing between mountain folds and valley folds to calculate the total fold density. This data allows me to compare different models statistically and see how simple geometric bases evolve into complex shapes.
